    161 + ### Payment Models: Fee-For-Service vs. Value-Based Care 162 +
    163 + The fee-for-service model (FFS) represents the traditional healthcare payment approach where providers receive reimbursement for each + individual service, procedure, test, or visit performed. Under FFS, a physician conducting a 15-minute office visit, ordering two lab + tests, and performing a minor procedure would bill separately for each component. This model creates problematic incentives: providers + generate more revenue by delivering more services, regardless of whether those services improve patient outcomes or represent the most + efficient care pathway. 164 +
    165 + Fee-for-service contributes to healthcare cost escalation through several mechanisms: 166 +
    167 + - Volume incentives: Providers are rewarded for quantity rather than quality of care 168 + - Fragmented care: Each specialist focuses on their narrow domain without coordinating across the patient's complete care needs 169 + - Overutilization: Financial incentives favor performing additional tests and procedures 170 + - Reactive rather than preventive: Payment occurs when patients are sick, not for keeping them healthy 171 + - Administrative burden: Each service requires separate coding, billing, and claims processing 172 +
    173 + Here's a comparison of the fundamental differences between payment models: 174 +
    175 + | Dimension | Fee-For-Service | Value-Based Care | 176 + |-----------|----------------|------------------| 177 + | Payment basis | Per service/procedure | Per patient or outcome | 178 + | Risk bearer | Payer assumes risk | Provider assumes partial/full risk | 179 + | Primary incentive | Maximize service volume | Improve outcomes, reduce costs | 180 + | Care coordination | Minimal | Essential | 181 + | Data requirements | Service codes, charges | Outcomes, quality metrics, costs | 182 + | Preventive care focus | Low | High | 183 + | Technology needs | Billing systems | Analytics, predictive models | 184 + | Provider mindset | "How many patients can I see?" | "How can I keep patients healthy?" | 185 +
    186 + Value-based care (VBC) represents a fundamental restructuring of healthcare economics, where providers receive compensation based on + patient health outcomes rather than service volume. Under value-based models, providers might receive a fixed payment per patient + (capitation), bonus payments for achieving quality benchmarks, or shared savings when they deliver care more efficiently than baseline + costs. This alignment of financial incentives with patient outcomes theoretically encourages providers to emphasize prevention, care + coordination, and evidence-based medicine. 187 +
    188 + Value-based care models include several variants: 189 +
    190 + - Pay-for-Performance (P4P): Bonus payments for meeting quality metrics 191 + - Bundled Payments: Single payment covering all services for an episode of care 192 + - Accountable Care Organizations (ACOs): Provider groups sharing responsibility for patient populations 193 + - Capitation: Fixed per-patient-per-month payment regardless of services consumed 194 + - Shared Savings/Risk: Providers share financial gains from cost reductions while maintaining quality 195 +
